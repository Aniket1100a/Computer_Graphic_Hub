Primitives and Attributes in Computer Graphics

Primitives

  • Points: Simplest primitive representing a single point in space.
  • Lines: Connects two points, forming a straight line segment.
  • Polygons: Closed shape formed by connecting multiple points with straight lines. Triangles and quadrilaterals are common polygon types.
  • Curves: Smooth, curved lines defined by control points. BÃ©zier curves and splines are examples of curves used in computer graphics.
  • 3D Solids: Geometric shapes such as spheres, cubes, cylinders, and cones in 3D space.

Attributes

  • Color: Specifies the color of the primitive. Colors can be represented using RGB values (Red, Green, Blue) or other color models.
  • Texture: A bitmap image applied to the surface of a primitive, adding detail and realism.
  • Shading: Determines how light interacts with the surface of a primitive. Common shading models include flat shading, Gouraud shading, and Phong shading.
  • Transparency: Specifies the opacity of the primitive, allowing objects to be partially see-through.
  • Reflection: Defines how much light is reflected off the surface of the primitive.
  • Refraction: Determines how light is bent as it passes through a transparent surface.
  • Specularity: Controls the intensity of specular highlights on the surface of the primitive.
  • Transformations: Apply transformations such as translation, rotation, and scaling to position and orient primitives in 2D or 3D space.
